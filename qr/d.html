Bug Hotels

Bug hotels are good places to find ladybirds, beetles, ants, alongside many other insects. In urban areas, where the primary natural habitat has been removed, bug hotels are a positive force in restoring the invertebrate population. Here, insects can lay their eggs, raise their young, and seek refuge from predators. 

Hibernation is an important stage of the life cycle of a bug with bug hotels providing a place to shelter when it gets cold. Many species like to hibernate in large groups but solitary species such as woodlice can take up residence in the stack. Hibernation spots are also provided by the natural woodland area, such as cracks in tree bark or rocks.

Natural Diversity

Creating a space for insects promotes natural diversity in the environment and also benefits many other species that live in the woodland. Hedgehogs eat many of the beetles and earthworms that are supported by the bug hotel. In addition to these bug-related benefits, these structures also promote sustainability through the use of both recycled and natural materials including old tiles, bricks, and reclaimed wood. 

Ladybirds

Ladybirds belong to the beetle family Coccinelliadae and prey on aphids (also known as green flies) and other small insects. There are 26 different species of ladybird in the UK and the woodland likely supports several of these species, each with a distinctive number of spots and colouring. 

Harlequin ladybirds (Harmonia axyridis) are one of the most familiar ladybird species and were introduced to Britain in the early 2000s. They have two common forms that can be easily identified - black with two red spots or orange with 18 black spots. The harlequin ladybird originates from Japan and was introduced used as a biological agent to control aphid populations. Ecologists feared that this also lead would also lead to the out-competition of other native aphid predators. However, the populations of native aphid predators, such as beetles and wasps, have remained stable throughout most of the UK. Thus, the harlequin ladybird is generally considered a beneficial species.

About Tree Walk

The vision of AESSEAL – and the vision of the Betterworld.Solutions project – is to find ways of making industry and the environment live in harmony. In parallel with a ‘factory of the future’ plan at the AESSEAL® Global Headquarters in Rotherham, England. AESSEAL® is developing ‘Tree Walk’ as an educational and public information resource.
