Mayflies

Mayflies are aquatic insects, common around freshwater wetlands. Ancestral traits in the division Palaeoptera, connect mayflies to dragonflies and damselflies, winged insects that are distinct from other lineages. Mayflies can be found around ponds and lakes, most commonly during the spring and summertime.

In some species, such as the common adult mayfly (Ephemera danica), their adult life stage only exists for one to two days. Contrarily, the nymph (larval) is the dominant stage of the mayfly’s lifecycle. Mayfly nymphs feed on detritus and other plant materials, found at the bottom of lakes or ponds.

Mayflies are keystone species and regulate primary production at the bottom of the food chain. Nymphs burrow down to forage, disturbing the sediment in the benthic zone. This process, known as bioturbation, reallocates and alters the nutrient availability in aquatic habitats. This movement of nutrients (nutrient cycling) is a supporting service to all ecosystem functions. Furthermore, mayfly nymphs provide food for species at higher trophic levels such as shrimp and fish.

Bioindicators

The absence or presence of a particular species can tell us a lot about the quality of the environment. Mayflies are one of the most commonly used indices of aquatic ecosystem health. As mayflies are detritus feeders, they are highly sensitive to any changes in the substrate or toxic substances entering the water. This means mayflies are only found at high-quality, minimally polluted sites and the presence of mayflies signals good water quality of a pond or stream.

Threats to mayflies

Mayflies rely on diversity in their environment and have specialised adaptations to exploit microhabitats. However, urbanisation has dramatically changed the landscape and converted many natural wetlands into urban or industrial zones. When this occurs, habitats are destroyed or fragmented, reducing the potential for diversity and is a threat to mayflies who inhabit niche terrains such as sandy lakes or river beds.

Other anthropogenic disturbances include the presence of pesticides in water bodies - a major risk to global freshwater biodiversity. Even at low concentrations, chemical pesticides can have detrimental impacts on mayfly populations. Sources of this pollution are often run-off from agriculture and industrial areas, which can be toxic or generate toxic by-products in aquatic ecosystems.

About Tree Walk

The vision of AESSEAL – and the vision of the Betterworld.Solutions project – is to find ways of making industry and the environment live in harmony. In parallel with a ‘factory of the future’ plan at the AESSEAL® Global Headquarters in Rotherham, England. AESSEAL® is developing ‘Tree Walk’ as an educational and public information resource.
