Hedgehogs

Hedgehogs are a familiar woodland mammal and are native to Britain. The name Hedgehog refers to the fact that they are often found in hedgerows, but they can be found in many other places too. These spiny mammals can be found in parks, gardens, farmland, and are often well-established in urban areas.

Hedgehogs are omnivores - mainly eating earthworms, beetles, and wild fruits. Adult hedgehogs have approximately 5000-7000 spines covering their body. Hedgehogs use their spines when threatened and curl into a ball to protect them against predators.

Hedgehogs are solitary, nocturnal animals and sleep for a large portion of the day in dens they dig underground. During the spring and summer time, they venture out at dusk to hunt for food. Although their eyesight is poor they have excellent hearing and a well-developed sense of smell so that they are able to forage.

Between November and mid-March are when hedgehogs hibernate, having built out their body fat throughout the autumn. During hibernation, hedgehogs are not really asleep but all their body functions have slowed so they can’t carry out their normal activity. By dropping their body temperature to match their surroundings, hedgehogs save energy and only move nests every few weeks.

Log Piles

Log piles help out hedgehogs by providing them with a suitable cool, dark environment in which to nest. Piles of different wood types simulate fallen trees that occur naturally in woodland and many species have adapted to use this habitat. Decaying wood is also of great value to many mosses, fungi, and invertebrate species. As log piles are teeming with insects, such as beetles, they also provide a source of food for hedgehogs and birds

Threats to Hedgehogs

The estimated population of hedgehogs in Britain is 1 million. However, this is half the size of the population in the 1990’s. Loss and fragmentation of habitat, road casualties, and the use of slug pellets, have all contributed to the decline of the hedgehog population. Hedgehogs tend to eat anything they can find so plastic litter, slug pellets, and other garden pesticides can all be toxic.

Providing a suitable habitat for hedgehogs, free from litter and other artificial materials, is important for their population. Woodlands afford important natural nesting sites, food sources, and a bit of peace and quiet perfect for hedgehog families. Pet food and water can also supplement a hedgehog’s regular diet and can be found in feeders around the tree walk.
