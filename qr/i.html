Woodland

The number of people with easy access to woodland has been declining in the UK, with forests only covering 12% of our land area. Bringing woodland areas to urban areas, closer to our homes, means they are more accessible and we are more likely to use them.

While walking wherever you are is a good way to be healthy and active, walking in nature is particularly beneficial. Studies have shown that woods can improve our physical health, mental well-being, and quality of life, greater than that from walking on a treadmill. This includes reducing our levels of stress hormones, lowering our blood pressure, and improving our energy levels.

Why do we need trees?

When situated near rivers or waterways, trees also reduce the volume of rainfall entering inlets, making them good flood defences. Trees mitigate soil erosion and reduce the amount of harmful pollutants from soil particles entering waterways.

Trees not only provide an enjoyable setting for us to exercise or relax but also sequester carbon dioxide. During photosynthesis, trees remove excess carbon dioxide and convert it into oxygen which is released into the atmosphere. This is important for maintaining an oxygen-rich atmosphere for us to breathe. The carbon-locking properties of trees are also crucial in the fight against climate change and these carbon stores need to be protected.

Tree Species

Lime, beech, birch, oak, and London plane, among other tree species, can be found in this woodland. London plane are one of the most efficient tree species in removing pollutants from the atmosphere and can be found in many cities around the world. These trees can filter toxins by trapping them in their leaves and bark. The bark flakes off to dispel pollutants, hence its distinctive camouflage-like pattern. Thus planting London plane helps to improve urban air quality.

Oak trees also add huge value to wildlife and were one of the first to colonise British woodlands. A single oak tree may live for almost 1,000 years and support more biodiversity than any other native plant. Over 2000 species of birds, mammals, fungi, invertebrates, and lichens use oak trees. Insects that live inside oak trees provide nutrition for higher up the food chain, such as birds, and their acorns provide food for squirrels and badgers.

About Tree Walk

The vision of AESSEAL – and the vision of the Betterworld.Solutions project – is to find ways of making industry and the environment live in harmony. In parallel with a ‘factory of the future’ plan at the AESSEAL® Global Headquarters in Rotherham, England. AESSEAL® is developing ‘Tree Walk’ as an educational and public information resource.
