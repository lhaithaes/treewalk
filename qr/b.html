Honey Bees

These wooden boxes are honey bee homes and in the summer can house up to 60,000 bees. As a social species, honey bees like to live in large colonies. These colonies are well-organised social groups where the division of labour is highly controlled. Every bee plays an important role in maintaining the health of the hive.

A colony typically consists of three types of adult bees: workers, drones, and a queen. Worker bees (young females) cooperate in nest building, food collection, and rearing of the young. They have specialised body structures including food glands and pollen baskets that allow them to perform their daily functions.

Drones (males) rely on worker bees for their food and it is the role of drones to fertilise the queen’s eggs. There is only one queen bee per colony and her primary function is reproduction. The queen produces the entire colony’s eggs and during the peak of her reproduction she can lay up to 1,500 eggs in a day. The queen also produces a chemical odour (pheromone) that gives each colony a distinctive marker – known as the queen’s substance. The quality of the queen’s substance determines many important aspects of the colony, such as its size, condition, and temperament. When the queen’s substance is no longer adequate, she is replaced by a worker bee with the specific genetic traits that can take over the hive. This highly coordinated social order and unique behaviour make honey bees one of the most fascinating species on earth.

Why Do We Need Bees

When worker bees collect food, using their specialised pollen baskets, they also transfer pollen between flowering plants. Cross-pollination occurs when pollen is spread from one flower to another of a certain species or variety. This allows plants to produce viable seeds and leads to strong genetic diversity.

Bees do not only pollinate wildflowers, making the landscape look more interesting, but over a third of global food crops. This means bees are essential in the productivity of the agro-food system, including apples, potatoes, beans, and many other fruits and vegetables we eat on a daily basis. These food stuffs allow us to gain nutrients such as vitamin A and iron from our diet. Therefore, by taking care of bees we are also taking care of ourselves too.

Impact of Climate Change and Habitat
Loss

There has been growing concern about the decline of bees. Since 1980, in the UK alone, 25 species of bee have gone extinct and there has been a 30% decline in the number of pollinators. The main drivers of this decline are habitat loss, exposure to pesticides, and the impacts of climate change. Sadly, urbanisation and intensive farming have caused the loss of pollinator friendly habitats. Bees now have fewer places to forage and are more exposed to chemical pesticides which are toxic to them. Compounding this, climate change is causing extreme weather events, such as droughts and storms, to become more frequent. Bees struggle to cope with these weather extremes, as they disrupt seasonal lifecycle ques and floral resource availability. The combined effects of climate change and habitat loss could have cascading impacts on pollination and ecosystem function.
