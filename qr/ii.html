Wildlife

The Tree Walk offers a stable habitat and good food supply to host all kinds of creatures, including some of the nation’s most loved animals.

It is crucial for us to conserve and protect our creatures for several reasons and by saving our creatures we ensure the long-term health and resilience of ecosystems, promote sustainable development and uphold responsibility to protect the natural world for present and future generations.

Foxes

Foxes are native to Britain and can be found all over the UK. They are most likely to be spotted at night when they are out hunting for food. Urban foxes in particular have become familiarised with humans, often permitting good views.

Foxes are scavengers, or ‘opportunistic omnivores’, eating almost anything they find. Their diet often consists of berries, birds, small mammals, insects, and discarded food left by humans. Although foxes forage independently, they live in family groups. Families reside in burrow systems, with dens commonly located under sheds, among tree roots, or in bushes. Fox cubs are often cared for by both parents, sometimes with the help of older siblings. Most foxes are born in the springtime in litters of four to five cubs.

Grey Squirrel 

Grey squirrels can be found all over Britain, but they are not in fact a native animal. The grey squirrel originates from North America and was introduced to the UK in the 19th century. The grey squirrel was brought over as an ornamental species to decorate the grounds of stately homes, and they rapidly adapted to this new habitat. However, the rapid spread of the grey squirrel damaged native flora and fauna. In particular, grey squirrel colonisation came to the detriment of the native red squirrel. Greys competed with reds for food, and also infected them with the squirrel pox virus. Whilst greys are immune to squirrel pox, it is deadly to reds. Red squirrels are now only found where grey squirrels are absent, such as in Scotland and the extensive pine forests of Northumberland and the Lake District.

Squirrels eat nuts, acorns, and tree seeds, which are abundant in woodlands. Squirrel nests, known as dreys, are where their young are reared. Dreys are made of twigs and branches and are built in trees above ground. The young squirrels live in the rest for around two months before they are ready to go out and forage for food. In the autumn, squirrels stash away food ready to be eaten in the winter, when they become less active. To prevent their stash from being stolen, squirrels may lead other squirrels close by off track by burying pretend stashes.

About Tree Walk

The vision of AESSEAL – and the vision of the Betterworld.Solutions project – is to find ways of making industry and the environment live in harmony. In parallel with a ‘factory of the future’ plan at the AESSEAL® Global Headquarters in Rotherham, England. AESSEAL® is developing ‘Tree Walk’ as an educational and public information resource.
